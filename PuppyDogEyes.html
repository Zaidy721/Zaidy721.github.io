Puppy Dog Eyes: Finding Influence in Pet Adoption

by Zaid Khalid

On September 16, 2017, my family adopted Zazu. Zazu, pictured above, is a brown mackerel tabby and was only 6 months young when he arrived at our house. The day before, a family friend of ours told us he was in need of a forever home after his original owners were suffering major allergy problems in his presence. My mother is the sole decision-maker in such matters decided she would brave her fears of anything on four legs and went to go find out what beast awaits her. When she arrived, she felt very little threat from the little guy whose attention span was smaller than his little toe. The next day he became our first four-legged family member. Exactly a year later, in her search for a companion for Zazu and for her own girl cat, mom rescued Zoya and Zeno from a local animal rescue. Like many families, Zoya was planned, but Zeno was not. Apparently, when mom went to take a look at her newest baby, she met eyes with Zeno and instantly fell in love. I was surprised. Not by his looks, but that his "puppy dog" eyes worked on her when mine usually don't.

So that's where the interest in this tutorial arose. I wanted to see just how likely it is for puppy dog eyes to affect adoption rates. Perhaps there are other factors that independently influence adoption. And if these factors do exist, are they increasing or decreasing with every year. I will perform my testing and analyses using data from Austin Animal Center (AAC) Shelter Intakes and Outcomes, a dataset courtesy of the AAC hosted on Kaggle. "The Austin Animal Center is the largest no-kill animal shelter in the United States that provides care and shelter to over 18,000 animals each year." The dataset presents approximately 80,000 shelter animal intakes and outcomes spanning from the end of 2013 to the beginning of 2018.

This tutorial will cover:

EDA and Visualization¶

According to the ASPCA, every year, 6.5 million pets are registered in U.S. animal shelters. Approximately 3.3 million dogs and 3.2 million cats. Although 1.5 million shelter animals are euthanized every year, double that figure are being adopted as well, 1.6 million dogs and 1.6 million cats. Since 2011, the number of euthanizations each year has decreased from 2.6 million. This is mostly due to a rise in shelter adoptions.

Let's see how true this is by measuring the frequency distribution of shelter adoptions per year.

Now let's try and see if we can incorporate a meaningful interaction term by factoring in the breeds. An interaction term is a parameter of a model that will tell if the independent features of the model have more of a combined effect on the regression.

Before we can jump right into it, we'll need to redefine our category for Breed with something more meaningful (aka numeric) that we can actually run through a model of best fit. One-hot encoding is the process of transforming a categorical variable into binary vectors and then using those vectors to perform numerical analysis, in our case for finding an interaction term for linear regression.

We will get our one-hot encoding of the Breed category by utilizing pandas' own get_dummies method. From there we'll combine our features of Age and Breed and fit them against the Adopted category to find out if the combination of the two has an effect on adoption.

Classification¶

Gradient descent is an optimization algorithm used to minimize a function by iteratively descending down the gradient of the said function. For our purpose, we'll be using gradient descent to minimize the cost needed to update our coefficients for a logistic regression model.

In [12]:
def logGD(X, y, T, alpha):
    
    # Create weights for model
    thetas = np.zeros(X.shape[1])
    
    for i in range(T):
    
        predictions = np.dot(X, thetas)

        # Sigmoid function on the predictions
        sigmoid = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(predictions)))

        # Calculate gradient by taking the average cost derivative of each feature and multiplying by learning rate
        gradient = alpha*(np.dot(X.T, (sigmoid - y))/len(X))

        # Subtract gradient from weights to minimize cost
        thetas = thetas -  gradient
        
    return thetas

Summarize Findings¶

To summarize what we've accomplished so far, we established a hypothesis and took to scraping a dense dataset to focus on the potential significant factors of adoption. We performed exploratory data analysis to find out about adoption trends and took a look at how age specifically trends in the adoption data. By standardizing our age and plotting adoption rates per value, we were able to find that young animals are just as likely as to be adopted as they are not adopted. However, there are still a greater amount of animals not being adopted than adopted. Although our search for an interaction term did not reveal anything significant about breed, our fit for linear regression did reveal how much the adoption rate drops per average unit increase in age. Finally, we were able to perform gradient descent to find the parameters of logistic regression.

The point of this guide was not to chastise those who adopt puppies or kittens. This is not a PSA for every reader to head to their nearest shelter and adopt the lot. Rather the point is to raise awareness on the state of adoption across the country. Several shelter animals who have been through traumatic experiences, illnesses, or abandonment are not given a chance because of the puppy dog eyes effect. Although we have not explained why it exists, it is no surprise that many adopting families and individuals can find the positives of adopting a younger animal. Reasons vary from the amount of space they take up, the various first-time experiences that can be shared, the care of small children, and more. If you are in a position to care for an animal in need, consider rescuing an older companion from your local shelter.
